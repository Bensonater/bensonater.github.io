Review: Emily is Away Too

Posted on 2020-02-17 Edited on 2020-02-18

There’s only so many ways to write a high-school drama.

It’s generally frowned upon to re-badge a merchandise under a different name and sell it pretending it’s something new, but if it’s nostalgia we eat it all up anyways. This is what’s going on with Emily is Away Too. Despite having a similar storyline and playstyle with the first game, the sequel couldn’t quite deliver the emotional impact of its predecessor and no amount of low-pixel avatars is going to redeem it.

One major factor for the magic of Emily is Away is that there are no happy endings in that game. No matter how desperate the player is at getting Emily back, she just doesn’t care anymore and decides to move on. It illustrates how friendships break down due to long distance or coming-of-age in real life. While Emily is Away Too definitely feels more like a choice-matters video game rather than a life lesson in this perspective, its endings are rather mundane and the storyline doesn’t subvert any expectations. It almost falls to the level of dating-sims and other visual novels, in that if you don’t upset Emily/Evelyn too much and play the nice-guy card, you ride into the sunset with her in a Disney-style ending.

The nostalgia of the game is still strong, especially with bands like Senses Fail and Linkin Park showing up for alternative music fans like me. However, there isn’t much more than that beyond the retro style interface of Emily is Away Too. There’s only so many ways to write a teenage high-school story, and unfortunately Emily is Away Too falls on the bottom half of the pile.

Verdict: 6 has-been pop punk bands out of 10

Emily is Away Too is available on Steam
